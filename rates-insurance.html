Our Philosophy

Why we're
out-of-network

We strive to provide the best individualized treatment plan for each client. Being out-of-network allows us to do that without insurance companies dictating your care.

When therapists are in-network, insurance companies may:

→

Require a Diagnosis

Insurance may require us to give you a diagnosis and justify that it's "severe enough" to warrant treatment.

→

Limit Your Sessions

Some insurance companies dictate how many sessions you're "allowed" to attend based on their criteria.

→

Access Your Information

Insurance companies may require access to personal information and session notes.

By staying out-of-network, your therapy stays between you and your therapist.

💡

"But wait—doesn't in-network mean I pay less?"

Not always. Many people are surprised to learn that being "in-network" doesn't guarantee lower costs. Read Emily's story to understand why.

Getting Reimbursed

How It Works

1

Check Your Benefits

Use the calculator above or call your insurance to ask about out-of-network mental health coverage.

2

Pay at Time of Service

You pay our standard rate at each session. We accept cards, including HSA/FSA.

3

Receive Your Superbill

We provide you with a superbill (itemized receipt) that includes all the info your insurance needs.

4

Get Reimbursed

Submit the superbill to your insurance and they reimburse you directly—often 40-80% of the session cost!

Questions to Ask Your Insurance

"Do I have out-of-network benefits for mental health services?"
"What is my out-of-network deductible?" (The amount you pay before insurance kicks in)
"What percentage do you reimburse for out-of-network mental health?"
"Is there a limit on the number of sessions covered per year?"
"How do I submit a claim for reimbursement?"

Your Rights

Good Faith Estimate

Under the No Surprises Act, you have the right to receive a "Good Faith Estimate" explaining how much your medical care will cost. This estimate includes the expected cost of any items or services that are reasonably expected to be provided together.

If you receive a bill that is at least $400 more than your Good Faith Estimate, you can dispute the bill. For questions or more information about your right to a Good Faith Estimate, contact us or visit cms.gov/nosurprises.

The Reality of "In-Network"

Emily's Story

The Scenario

Emily starts therapy in October at an in-network therapy practice. She feels relieved: "They take my insurance!"

New year arrives. Deductible resets. Emily returns for her therapy in January—same practice, same provider.

What Emily doesn't realize:

Her deductible reset on January 1st. Her deductible is $5,000.

Until she personally pays $5,000 out-of-pocket in covered medical expenses, insurance does not begin sharing the cost.

The Surprise

Even though her therapist is in-network, Emily is responsible for the full contracted rate per session until she hits that deductible.

Here's what practices often don't explain: The charge that goes to insurance is typically at a higher rate. The balance gets "written off" as part of the contracted rate—but only after you hit your deductible. Before that? The practice continues charging the higher rate, and you're responsible for all of it.

This surprises many people. Will Emily's care be disrupted?

The Part Most People Don't Know

Here's where it gets complicated. The therapist is contractually bound by their agreement with the insurance company. That contract determines:

  • The exact rate the therapist is allowed to charge
  • The therapist cannot reduce fees
  • The therapist cannot waive balances
  • The therapist cannot offer a sliding scale

Therapists cannot alter that rate without violating their insurance contract. Now both client and therapist are in a bind.

💡 The takeaway: "In-network" doesn't always mean cheaper—and it often means less flexibility. Being out-of-network allows us to work directly with you on what makes sense for your situation.
